# What to Feed Composting Worms (And What to Avoid)

**Last Updated: April 2026 | WormGarden.com**

So you have got a bin full of red wigglers, and now you are standing in your kitchen holding a banana peel, wondering: *Can my worms eat this?*

You are not alone. Knowing what to feed composting worms is the single most common question new vermicomposters ask -- and getting it right makes the difference between a thriving, odor-free worm bin and a smelly, frustrating mess.

This guide gives you everything you need: a complete worm food list of what worms can eat, what to keep out, how much to feed, and what to do when things go sideways. Bookmark it, print it, tape it to your fridge. Your worms will thank you.

## The Golden Rule: Balance Your Browns and Greens

Before we get into the full list of vermicomposting food scraps, you need to understand one simple concept: **carbon-to-nitrogen balance**.

Worms need two types of food:

- **Greens (nitrogen-rich):** Fruit and vegetable scraps, coffee grounds, fresh plant trimmings. These are the nutrient-dense foods that fuel microbial activity and give your worms the protein they need.
- **Browns (carbon-rich):** Shredded cardboard, newspaper, dried leaves, egg cartons. These provide energy, absorb excess moisture, and create airflow in the bin.

A healthy worm bin runs on roughly a **3:1 ratio of browns to greens** by volume. That means for every handful of kitchen scraps you add, toss in about three handfuls of shredded cardboard or newspaper on top.

Think of it like building a sandwich. The browns are the bread; the greens are the filling. Too much filling and everything gets soggy and sour. Too much bread and the worms run out of the good stuff.

When you get this balance right, your bin will smell like fresh earth -- nothing more.

## The Complete YES List: What Worms Love to Eat

Here is your go-to worm food list. Everything below is safe, nutritious, and worm-approved.

### Fruits

- **Banana peels** -- A worm favorite. Chop them up to speed decomposition.
- **Apple cores and peels** -- Break them into smaller pieces first.
- **Melon rinds** -- Watermelon, cantaloupe, honeydew. Worms go wild for these.
- **Berries** -- Strawberry tops, mushy blueberries, raspberry remnants.
- **Grapes** -- Cut in half or let them soften before adding.
- **Pears, peaches, plums** -- Remove pits first; they will not break down.
- **Avocado flesh** -- Skip the pit and the tough skin.

**Pro tip:** Limit citrus fruits like oranges, lemons, and limes to small amounts. A little is fine, but too much raises the acidity in your bin and can irritate your worms' skin. More on that in the NO list below.

### Vegetables

- **Lettuce and leafy greens** -- Decompose quickly; worms love them.
- **Cucumber ends and peels** -- High moisture content, easy to eat.
- **Carrot peels and tops** -- Chop into small pieces.
- **Squash and zucchini** -- Soft and worm-friendly.
- **Bell pepper scraps** -- The flesh is great; go easy on seeds.
- **Pumpkin** -- After Halloween, your jack-o-lantern becomes worm food.
- **Cooked plain vegetables** -- No butter, oil, or seasoning.
- **Corn cobs** -- They will break down, but slowly. Chop them small.

### Coffee and Tea

- **Coffee grounds** -- Excellent nitrogen source. Worms seem genuinely attracted to them. Add filter and all.
- **Tea bags** -- Remove the staple if there is one. Paper-based tea bags break down nicely. Avoid nylon mesh bags.
- **Loose leaf tea** -- Toss it straight in.

### Grains and Starches

- **Plain cooked rice or pasta** -- Small amounts only, no sauces.
- **Stale bread and crackers** -- Moisten first. Avoid anything with heavy seasoning.
- **Oatmeal** -- Plain, unsweetened.

### Bedding Materials (Browns)

- **Shredded cardboard** -- Corrugated boxes, toilet paper rolls, cereal boxes (remove any glossy coating).
- **Newspaper** -- Shred it into strips. Modern newspaper ink is soy-based and safe.
- **Egg cartons** -- Tear into small pieces. Excellent brown material.
- **Brown paper bags** -- Shredded or torn.
- **Dried leaves** -- Crumble them for faster breakdown.
- **Dryer lint** -- Only from natural fiber clothing (cotton, linen). Skip if you wear a lot of synthetics.

### Other Worm-Safe Foods

- **Crushed eggshells** -- Do not skip these. They add calcium and help regulate pH in the bin. Rinse, dry, and crush them fine.
- **Hair and nail clippings** -- Slow to decompose, but perfectly safe.
- **Cotton and wool scraps** -- Must be 100% natural fiber; no blends.

## The Complete NO List: What to Keep Out of Your Worm Bin

Not everything that comes out of your kitchen belongs in the worm bin. These items cause odor problems, attract pests, or can harm your worms outright.

### Meat, Fish, and Bones

- **Why not:** These decompose slowly, produce strong odors, and attract rats, raccoons, and flies. Meat also generates harmful bacteria as it rots. Keep it out -- no exceptions.

### Dairy Products

- **Why not:** Cheese, milk, yogurt, and butter all go rancid in a worm bin. They create foul smells and draw pests faster than almost anything else.

### Oils, Grease, and Fatty Foods

- **Why not:** Fats coat the bin environment and reduce airflow. They also create anaerobic pockets that smell terrible and can suffocate worms. This includes salad dressing, fried foods, and buttery scraps.

### Citrus in Excess

- **Why not:** Small amounts of orange peel are fine. But large quantities of citrus -- lemons, limes, grapefruit -- spike acidity levels. The compound d-limonene in citrus peel is also a natural insecticide, and in high concentrations it can harm your worm population. Keep citrus to under 10% of total food scraps.

### Onions and Garlic

- **Why not:** Both are highly acidic and contain compounds that worms actively avoid. A small piece buried in a scrap mix will not cause a disaster, but do not make a habit of it. If your worms are migrating away from one corner of the bin, check for onion scraps.

### Spicy and Heavily Seasoned Foods

- **Why not:** Hot peppers, salsa, curries, and anything with heavy spices will irritate your worms. Capsaicin is a chemical deterrent to most organisms, worms included.

### Pet Waste (Dog and Cat Feces)

- **Why not:** Dog and cat waste can contain harmful parasites, bacteria, and pathogens (including toxoplasmosis from cats) that are not reliably eliminated through vermicomposting. The castings could contaminate your garden. Rabbit and hamster droppings from herbivore pets, on the other hand, are generally safe in small quantities.

### Glossy or Coated Paper

- **Why not:** Magazines, glossy flyers, and coated cardboard may contain plastics, heavy metals in inks, or chemical coatings that do not belong in your compost.

## How Much to Feed (And How Often)

Here is where many beginners go wrong: **overfeeding**. It is the number-one mistake in vermicomposting, and it leads to bad smells, fruit flies, and unhappy worms.

### The Half-Body-Weight Rule

A healthy population of red wigglers can eat roughly **half their body weight in food per day**. If you started your bin with one pound of worms, that means about half a pound of food scraps daily.

But do not take this as a hard target. It is a ceiling, not a goal. In practice, most bins do well with feeding **two to three times per week**.

### The Handful Test

Not sure if your worms are ready for more food? Try this:

1. Push aside the bedding where you last added scraps.
2. If the previous feeding is mostly gone -- just dark, crumbly material left -- it is time to feed again.
3. If you still see recognizable food scraps, wait another day or two.

This simple check prevents overfeeding better than any schedule or formula.

### Pocket Feeding

Rather than spreading food across the entire surface, try **pocket feeding**: bury scraps in a different corner of the bin each time you feed. This gives worms a fresh zone to work through and lets previously fed areas finish breaking down. If you are using a stacking tray system like the [AFFILIATE: Worm Factory 360], this rotation happens naturally as you move between trays.

### The Freezing Trick

Before feeding, consider tossing your kitchen scraps into a bag in the freezer for at least 24 hours. When you thaw them out, two things happen:

1. **The cell walls break down**, making the food softer and easier for worms to consume.
2. **Fruit fly eggs are killed**, dramatically reducing pest problems in your bin.

This one habit eliminates most fruit fly issues. It is the single best tip experienced vermicomposters pass along to beginners.

## Troubleshooting: Why Are My Worms Not Eating?

You added food three days ago and it looks completely untouched. What is going on? Here are the most common reasons -- and what to do about each.

### The Bin Is Too Cold

Worms are most active between **55 and 77 degrees Fahrenheit (13 to 25 degrees Celsius)**. Below 50 degrees, their metabolism slows to a crawl. If your bin is in an unheated garage in winter, move it somewhere warmer or reduce feeding significantly until temperatures rise.

### The Bin Is Too Wet

A waterlogged bin becomes anaerobic -- meaning oxygen levels drop, bad bacteria take over, and worms head for drier ground (or try to escape). Squeeze a handful of bedding: it should feel like a wrung-out sponge. If water drips freely, add dry shredded cardboard or newspaper to soak up the excess.

### Food Pieces Are Too Large

Worms do not have teeth. They rely on microorganisms to soften food before they can eat it. Large chunks of carrot or whole banana peels take much longer to break down than chopped or blended scraps. Cut food into roughly one-inch pieces, or try the freezing method mentioned above.

### You Are Overfeeding

If uneaten food is piling up, the answer is simple: stop feeding for a week. Let the worms catch up. Then resume with smaller amounts. A well-managed bin should never have more than one feeding's worth of visible food at a time.

### The Food Is Too Acidic

An overload of citrus, tomatoes, or pineapple can drop the pH and make the bin inhospitable. Add a generous dusting of crushed eggshells or a sprinkle of agricultural lime to buffer the acidity, and cut back on acidic scraps going forward.

### New Worms Need Adjustment Time

If you just set up your bin, give your worms a week to settle in before their first real feeding. They are stressed from shipping and handling. Start with a small amount of soft food -- a few banana peels or some melon -- and let them acclimate.

## Going on Vacation? Plan Ahead

Heading out of town for a week or two? Your worms will be fine with a little preparation.

- **Feed a generous portion** of slow-decomposing foods before you leave: chunks of pumpkin, whole avocado halves (flesh only), or thick slices of watermelon rind.
- **Add extra bedding** -- a thick layer of moist shredded cardboard on top acts as both food and moisture control.
- **Consider a worm chow supplement** like the one from [AFFILIATE: Uncle Jim's Worm Chow]. Sprinkle it on the bedding before you leave; it provides a slow, steady food source while you are away.

Most healthy bins can go two to three weeks without attention. Just make sure the bedding is moist (not wet) and the bin is in a temperature-stable location.

## Feed Smart, Compost Happy

Getting your worm food list dialed in is not complicated once you know the basics. Stick to fruit and vegetable scraps, keep your browns-to-greens ratio in check, resist the urge to overfeed, and freeze your scraps before tossing them in. Do that consistently and your worm bin will reward you with rich, dark castings that your garden will love.

The beautiful thing about vermicomposting is that it is forgiving. If you make a mistake -- and everyone does early on -- your worms will let you know. Adjust, learn, and keep going.

Happy composting.
