## 5. DIY Rubbermaid Bin Setup -- Budget Pick

**Price:** $10-15 | **Buy from:** Any hardware or big-box store

You do not need a purpose-built product to start worm composting. Two nested 10-gallon Rubbermaid or Sterilite storage bins, a drill, and 15 minutes of your time can produce a perfectly functional worm bin. This is how many of us -- the WormGarden editorial team included -- started vermicomposting.

**Setup Overview:**
- Drill approximately 20-30 small holes (1/8") in the lid and upper sides of the inner bin for ventilation
- Drill 4-6 slightly larger holes (1/4") in the bottom of the inner bin for drainage
- Nest the inner bin inside the outer bin (the outer bin catches leachate)
- Add moist bedding, food scraps, and worms

**Specifications:**
- Dimensions: Varies by bin (typical: 22" x 16" x 8")
- Weight: Under 3 lbs empty
- Capacity: 10-18 gallons depending on bin chosen
- Material: Polypropylene plastic

**Pros:**
- Incredibly cheap -- under $15 for the entire setup
- Available at virtually any store
- Great for experimenting before investing in a purpose-built bin
- Easily replaceable if damaged

**Cons:**
- Poor airflow compared to purpose-built bins; requires more monitoring
- Harvesting is messy and time-consuming (dump-and-sort method)
- Prone to moisture buildup, odors, and anaerobic conditions without careful management
- No upward-migration system -- you must manually separate worms from castings
- Not particularly attractive; most people do not want this in their kitchen

**Verdict:** The DIY Rubbermaid bin is the best way to test whether vermicomposting is right for you without any real financial commitment. However, expect more hands-on maintenance and a less pleasant harvesting experience. Most people who stick with the hobby upgrade within 6-12 months. ## Buying Guide: What to Look for in a Worm Bin

Choosing the best worm bin comes down to five key factors. Here is what actually matters and what you can safely ignore.

### 1. Bin Type: Stacking Trays vs. Flow-Through vs. Single Compartment

- **Stacking tray systems** (Worm Factory 360, VermiHut Plus, Hot Frog) use upward migration. When the bottom tray is full of castings, worms move up to the food in the next tray. Harvesting is relatively clean.
- **Flow-through systems** (Hungry Bin, Urban Worm Bag) let you harvest from the bottom while feeding from the top. Less handling of worms, but the bin designs vary widely in quality.
- **Single compartment bins** (DIY Rubbermaid) require you to manually separate worms from castings at harvest time. Functional but messy.

For most people, a stacking tray or flow-through system is worth the investment.

### 2. Airflow and Ventilation

This is the single most important factor in worm bin success. Inadequate airflow leads to anaerobic decomposition, which produces foul odors and can kill your worms. Look for bins with ventilation holes, breathable materials, or well-designed tray gaps. The Urban Worm Bag excels here; solid plastic bins with small drilled holes (like DIY setups) are the most prone to problems.

### 3. Size and Capacity

A good rule of thumb: one pound of red wigglers can process roughly half a pound of food scraps per day. A household of two typically produces about 2-3 lbs of compostable food waste per week, so a standard 4-tray bin or medium-sized flow-through handles this easily. Larger families or serious gardeners should consider the Hungry Bin's higher capacity.

### 4. Indoor vs. Outdoor Use

If your bin will live outdoors, prioritize UV-resistant materials, a secure lid for pest control, and weather-durable construction. The Maze Worm Farm and Hungry Bin are the best outdoor choices in this roundup. For indoor use, focus on compact size, odor control, and aesthetics.

### 5. Ease of Harvesting

Harvesting is where cheap bins become frustrating. The dump-and-sort method required by DIY bins is messy and time-consuming. Stacking trays and flow-through designs dramatically reduce the effort involved. If you plan to compost long-term, invest in a bin with a smart harvesting system.

### What You Can Ignore

- **"Worm tea" marketing**: The liquid that drains from worm bins (leachate) is not the same as properly brewed worm tea. Spigots are nice for drainage, but do not choose a bin based on "tea" claims.
- **Fancy accessories**: Most included add-ons (scrapers, "worm food" packets) are unnecessary. Your kitchen scraps and shredded cardboard are all you need.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the best worm composting bin for beginners?

The **Worm Factory 360** is our top recommendation for beginners. Its stackable tray system is intuitive, the included starter materials get you going immediately, and there is a massive online community for support when you run into issues. If budget is a concern, the **Urban Worm Bag** at $50-80 is an excellent beginner-friendly alternative. For a complete walkthrough, check our [worm composting for beginners guide][LINK: Worm Composting for Beginners Guide].

### How many worms do I need to start?

We recommend starting with 1 lb of red wigglers (approximately 800-1,000 worms). This is enough for a standard-sized bin and allows the population to establish before you begin feeding at full volume. You can order them from [Uncle Jim's Worm Farm][AFFILIATE: uncle-jims-worm-farm], which ships live worms year-round with arrival guarantees. For more on worm species, see our guide on [red wigglers vs nightcrawlers][LINK: Red Wigglers vs Nightcrawlers].

### Do worm bins smell bad?

A properly maintained worm bin should smell like fresh earth -- nothing more. If your bin stinks, it is almost always a sign of overfeeding, poor airflow, or excess moisture. Bins with better ventilation, like the Urban Worm Bag, are the most forgiving on this front. Enclosed plastic bins require more careful feeding and moisture management.

### Can I keep a worm bin indoors?

Absolutely. Many of the bins in this roundup -- the Worm Factory 360, Urban Worm Bag, VermiHut Plus, and Hot Frog -- work well indoors. Worms are quiet, a healthy bin has no odor, and the bins are compact enough to fit under a kitchen sink, in a closet, or on a laundry room shelf. We kept bins indoors for months during testing with zero complaints.

### How often do I harvest worm castings?

In a stacking tray system, the bottom tray is typically ready to harvest every 3-4 months. Flow-through bins like the Hungry Bin and Urban Worm Bag allow for more continuous harvesting. You will know the castings are ready when the material is dark, crumbly, and no longer recognizable as the food or bedding you added.

### Is a DIY worm bin worth it?

For testing the waters, yes. A $10-15 DIY Rubbermaid setup is the lowest-risk way to find out if worm composting is for you. But most people who stick with vermicomposting upgrade to a purpose-built bin within a year because of the dramatically easier harvesting and better moisture and airflow management. Think of it as a starter option, not a long-term solution.

### What should I feed my worms?

Red wigglers thrive on fruit and vegetable scraps, coffee grounds and filters, tea bags, crushed eggshells, and shredded cardboard or newspaper. Avoid citrus in large quantities, onions, garlic, meat, dairy, oily foods, and pet waste. A good feeding rhythm is small amounts every 2-3 days rather than a large dump once a week.
